Shin conditioning. For fighters in MMA,
shin conditioning is the foundation of
delivering powerful kicks without
breaking under impact. The process works
through controlled, repeated trauma.
When a shin strikes a hard surface,
microfractures form in the tibia. The
body responds by flooding the area with
cells that deposit new calcium and
collagen. Over weeks and months, this
thickens the bone's outer layer, making
it denser and more resistant to future
impacts. The result is a shin that can
absorb and deliver strikes with far less
pain. Shin conditioning also reduces
fight injuries. A conditioned shin can
check an opponent's kick without
fracturing, turning defense into offense
by damaging the attacker's leg. Fighters
who skip this training often suffer
stress fractures or debilitating pain
from blocked kicks. The adaptation is
not just physical. Pain tolerance
increases as nerve endings are
repeatedly damaged and desensitized.
What once felt unbearable becomes
manageable, allowing fighters to throw
full-force kicks without hesitation.
This psychological shift is as valuable
as the bone density itself. The most
common method is kicking a heavy bag.
Fighters start with light taps,
gradually increasing force as the bone
adapts. Some fighters use rolling
sticks, hardwood or metal rods pressed
along the shin with increasing pressure.
This creates microdamage to the thin
membrane covering the bone, deadening
the nerves and reducing pain
sensitivity. The practice leaves visible
bruising, but over time, the shin
becomes less responsive to pain while
the bone grows harder. Tony Ferguson's
steel pipe routine to an extreme. By
kicking a metal pole, he forces maximum
bone stress with every strike. Neck
training. For MMA fighters, a thick,
powerful neck serves two critical
purposes, reducing the risk of knockouts
and dominating in grappling exchanges.
By stabilizing the head and limiting how
much it snaps back or sideways, the neck
reduces the brain's movement inside the
skull. Fighters with conditioned necks
can take shots that would drop others
simply because their head doesn't whip
as violently on impact. The neck also
plays a central role in grappling.
Fighters who neglect this area find
themselves controlled easily, their
heads manipulated by opponents who can
crank submissions or drive them to the
mat. Neck bridges are the most effective
method. The wrestler's bridge involves
lying on your back, arching upward until
only the head and feet touch the ground,
then holding the position or rocking
side to side. It forces the neck muscles
to support the entire body weight,
building strength and endurance
simultaneously. Neck curls provide a
more controlled alternative. Fighters
lie on a bench with their head hanging
off the edge, then use a weight plate
held against the forehead or back of the
head to perform slow, deliberate curls.
Some use specialized neck harnesses that
attach weights and allow
multi-directional training, hitting the
sides of the neck as well. Core
training. For MMA fighters, core
strength is not about aesthetics. It's
about surviving body attacks and
generating power in every movement. When
a liver shot or body kick lands, the
force compresses internal organs and
shocks the nervous system. A weak core
offers no protection. A conditioned core
acts as armor. Core training also powers
every strike and takedown. Punches don't
originate in the arms, they start in the
hips and transfer through the core
before exploding out through the fist. A
weak midsection leaks energy, turning
what should be knockout power into arm
punches. The same applies to sprawls and
explosive movements. The core connects
the upper and lower body, and its
strength determines how efficiently
force transfers between them. Planks and
their variations build foundational
endurance. Front planks, side planks,
and hollow body positions for extended
periods, forcing the core to stabilize
under sustained tension. Sit-ups and leg
raises target the rectus abdominis
directly. Fighters perform high-rep
sets, often weighted or performed on a
decline bench to increase resistance.
Body hardening drills prepare the core
for actual impacts. Fighters have
training partners throw medicine balls
into their stomach repeatedly, or they
take controlled body shots while
flexing. This conditions both the
muscles and the pain tolerance, teaching
the body to reflexively brace when
attacked. The adaptation is rapid
compared to bone conditioning. Core
muscles respond to high-frequency
training, and fighters often work the
midsection five to six days per week.
The muscles recover quickly, allowing
volume to accumulate without
overtraining. Hard sparring. Hard
sparring replicates what no drill can,
the unpredictable chaos of getting hit
by someone trying to hurt you. Bag work
and mitt routines are controlled. Shadow
boxing has no resistance. Only hard
sparring forces a fighter to defend,
counter, and survive while absorbing
real impact. This builds physical
resilience and mental conditioning
simultaneously. The body learns to take
shots without panicking. The mind stays
calm under fire. Experienced fighters
instinctively tighten their core, tuck
their chin, and roll with punches the
moment they sense impact. These reflexes
cannot be taught through light work,
they develop only under real threat.
Hard sparring also sharpens timing and
distance control in ways that technical
drills cannot. Against a partner
throwing measured punches, a fighter can
develop bad habits, relying on
predictable rhythms. Against someone
trying to land hard, every movement
matters. Footwork tightens. Head
movement becomes instinctive. The
fighter learns to read incoming strikes
and react in real time, not rehearsed
time. But the danger is accumulation.
Too much hard sparring leads to brain
trauma, declining reflexes, and
premature decline. The same toughness
they build becomes the tool that
destroys them. Smart fighters limit hard
sparring to specific phases of camp.
Many reserve it for the final weeks
before a fight, using lighter technical
work earlier to sharpen skills without
damage. Pereira's background in Glory
kickboxing, where sparring culture is
notoriously brutal, gave him the ability
to stay composed while eating heavy
shots. His fights reflect this. He has
the ability to fight in close quarters
without taking too much damage.
Strickland's sparring footage shows the
same principle. Cardio training. For MMA
fighters, superior conditioning is not
just about lasting longer. It's a weapon
that turns the fight into a war of
attrition, where the tougher
cardiovascular system wins. When a
fighter's cardio breaks, everything
collapses. Punches lose power, defense
becomes sluggish, the mind starts
negotiating, looking for a way out
instead of a way to win. Road work runs
are the foundation. Fighters log three
to six miles at a steady pace, building
the aerobic base that allows sustained
effort over long periods. This slow,
controlled running improves the body's
ability to convert oxygen into usable
energy. The heart strengthens, stroke
volume increases, and the cardiovascular
system becomes more efficient at
delivering oxygen to working muscles.
Hill sprints add explosive conditioning.
Fighters charge up steep inclines in
short bursts of maximum effort, then
recover on the walk down before
repeating. The incline forces greater
recruitment of fast-twitch muscle
fibers, building the leg drive needed
for takedowns and explosive strikes.
Over time, the body adapts by buffering
lactic acid more efficiently, allowing
fighters to unleash bursts of activity
without gassing out. Altitude training
takes conditioning to another level.
Fighters like Cain Velasquez famously
trained in the mountains of California,
where thin air forces the body to adapt
by producing more red blood cells. These
cells carry oxygen to muscles, and the
increased count means greater endurance
when the fighter returns to sea level.
The lungs work harder, the heart pumps
more efficiently, and the entire system
becomes more resilient under fatigue.
Sparring is the most specific cardio
tool. Unlike running or hill work,
sparring demands energy output that
mirrors an actual fight. Explosive
bursts of strikes, grappling exchanges,
constant movement, and defensive
reactions. High-volume sparring sessions
that extend beyond normal fight length
condition both the aerobic and anaerobic
systems simultaneously. The
unpredictable pace taxes the
cardiovascular system in ways no drill
can replicate, forcing adaptations that
carry directly into competition. The
mental toughness built through cardio
work is as valuable as the physical
adaptation. Pushing through fatigue
during long runs or brutal hill sprints
teaches fighters to ignore discomfort
and keep moving. This mental resilience
transfers directly to fights. When the
body screams to quit in the third round,
fighters who have broken through that
barrier in training can push past it
again. Breath control. For MMA fighters,
breath control is the
stress, and
erupts. Every punch forces an
exhalation. Every defensive movement
requires precise timing between breath
and motion. When a fighter loses control
of their breathing, the heart rate
spikes, oxygen delivery becomes
inefficient, and panic sets in. Ragged,
desperate gasps signal to both the body
and the opponent that the fighter is
breaking. Controlled breathing does the
opposite. It regulates heart rate,
maximizes oxygen intake, and projects
calm even under extreme duress. Nasal
breathing during low-intensity work
trains efficiency. Fighters practice
breathing exclusively through the nose
during road work or technical drills,
forcing the body to extract more oxygen
from each breath. The practice filters
and humidifies air more effectively than
mouth breathing, reducing respiratory
strain during long training sessions.
Timed exhalations during combinations
stabilize the core and maintain rhythm.
Fighters learn to exhale sharply with
each punch, syncing breath with
movement. This prevents breath holding,
which causes oxygen debt. Paced recovery
breaths between exchanges are critical.
After a flurry or a hard grappling
scramble, experienced fighters take
controlled breaths to flood their system
with oxygen and lower their heart rate.
This active recovery keeps them sharp
for the next exchange, instead of
spiraling into oxygen debt. Box
breathing and tactical breathing calm
the nervous system. Fighters use a
simple pattern. Inhale for four counts,
hold for four, exhale for four, hold for
four. This activates the parasympathetic
nervous system, lowering heart rate and
reducing cortisol. Many fighters use
this technique backstage before walking
out, transforming pre-fight anxiety into
focused calm. The mental component is
equally important. Controlled breathing
prevents the cascade into panic. Instead
of spiraling, they can reset their
nervous system with deliberate breaths,
staying present and functional.
