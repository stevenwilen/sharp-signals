Jones vs. Reyes. Jon Jones had defended
his light heavyweight title multiple
times and was considered one of the
greatest UFC fighters in history. Going
into UFC 247, most expected him to
handle Dominick Reyes without too much
trouble. That's not what happened. From
the start, Reyes came out aggressive. He
was pressuring Jones, landing clean
combinations, and doing something nobody
had really done before, making Jones
look uncomfortable. The body kicks were
landing, his boxing was sharp, and for
the first three rounds, Reyes was
controlling the fight. At one point in
the second round, Jones actually turned
and moved away from Reyes's pressure,
which was unusual to see from someone of
his caliber. By round three, Reyes was
winning the striking battle. The stats
would later show that he landed 116
significant strikes compared to Jones's
104. The first time in Jones's 22-fight
career, he'd been outstruck. Reyes
looked like he was on his way to pulling
off the upset, but championship fights
are five rounds for a reason. Rounds
four and five saw a shift. Jones started
mixing in his wrestling, secured a
couple of takedowns, and turned up the
pressure. Reyes was visibly tired, and
Jones was doing what champions do,
finding ways to win late in fights. When
the final bell rang, both fighters
thought they'd won. The crowd was
buzzing, then came the decision.
Unanimous decision for Jon Jones, 48-47,
48-47, and 49-46.
That last scorecard stood out
immediately. Judge Joe Solis scored it
49-46,
giving Jones four rounds in a fight
where most people thought Reyes clearly
won the first three. The boos from the
Houston crowd were immediate and loud.
Here's where it gets interesting. Dana
White, the UFC president, said at the
post-fight press conference that he
scored it three rounds to two in Reyes's
favor. When your own boss disagrees with
the judges' decision to crown your
biggest star, that tells you something.
The media agreed. 14 out of the 21 media
members scoring at ringside had Reyes
winning. Commentators were openly
critical. Joe Rogan called the 49-46
scorecard insane. Dominick Cruz said
that Judge Joe Solis should have his
judging card revoked. Reyes was
understandably frustrated after the
fight. He believed he'd done enough to
win and made Jones look beatable for the
first time in years. The problem was, he
never got a chance to run it back. Jones
moved on, eventually vacated the title
to move to heavyweight, and Reyes went
on a tough three-fight losing streak by
knockout. His career trajectory changed
completely after that night. O'Malley
vs. Yan. Going into this fight, O'Malley
was a massive underdog. At one point,
the betting lines had Yan as nearly a
three-to-one favorite. On paper, it made
sense. Yan was a former bantamweight
champion, ranked number one in the
division, and coming off tough losses
that he was looking to bounce back from.
O'Malley, he wasn't even ranked top 10.
Dana White had made it clear before the
fight, the winner would get the next
title shot. That made the stakes even
higher. From the opening bell, Yan
pressed forward. He was attacking
O'Malley's legs, backing him up, and
looking like the favorite everyone
expected. Most people watching thought
Yan took the first round. He secured a
takedown near the end and looked in
control. The second round got violent.
O'Malley came out and rocked Yan twice
with sharp left hands. For a moment, it
looked like he might finish the fight,
but Yan responded with a massive
overhand that stunned O'Malley right
back. From there, Yan went to his
wrestling, landing multiple takedowns
and spending significant time on top. By
the end of round two, most observers
felt Yan was up two rounds to none. Then
came the third round. O'Malley landed a
knee that cut Yan badly above his right
eye. The momentum shifted. O'Malley was
landing strikes. Yan was bleeding, but
the former champion kept pushing
forward. He continued shooting takedowns
in the final minutes, trying to secure
the round with grappling control. The
fight went the distance. When the
decision was announced, it was a split
decision for Sean O'Malley. Two judges
scored it 29-28 for O'Malley. One judge
had it 29-28 for Yan. The MMA community
erupted. Petr Yan tweeted immediately
after the fight, &amp;quot;F the judges.&amp;quot; Fellow
fighters piled on. Henry Cejudo called
it the worst robbery in UFC history.
Even O'Malley seemed unsure after the
fight. When asked if he thought he won,
he said he wanted to watch the fight
back before deciding. He admitted that
he didn't even know if he won and
thought that it could have gone either
way. And Khabib thought Yan won all
three rounds. GSP vs. Hendricks. UFC 167
was supposed to be a celebration, the
company's 20th anniversary, Georges
St-Pierre breaking records. Instead, it
turned into one of the most
controversial decisions in UFC history.
GSP came into this fight on 11-fight win
streak. He was the heavy favorite at
minus 260. Johny Hendricks, a two-time
NCAA wrestling champion with knockout
power, was the underdog at plus 200. On
paper, it was supposed to be another
successful title defense for the
Canadian legend. The fight started, and
it became clear early that Hendricks
wasn't intimidated. GSP secured a quick
takedown in the first round and
attempted a guillotine choke, but
Hendricks fought it off. Then Hendricks
started landing hard punches, elbows
against the cage, a left knee. The round
was competitive, but Hendricks looked
comfortable and was marking up the
champion's face. Round two is where
things got serious. Hendricks came out
smiling and immediately went to work. He
cracked GSP with a massive left hand
that made spit fly out of the champion's
mouth. Then another uppercut. GSP was
wobbled twice in the round, stumbling
and trying to survive. Hendricks landed
37 total strikes to GSP's 30, with 19
being power shots compared to just the
seven from the champion. GSP's face was
cut and swelling, and it was a clear
10-9 for Hendricks. The third round saw
GSP settle down. He found the range with
his jab, landed kicks, and controlled
the pace better. It wasn't dominant, but
he did enough to take the round. Round
four swung back to Hendricks. He landed
41 total strikes to GSP's 24. The fifth
and final round went to GSP. He landed
two takedowns and found success in the
clinch. Hendricks was still dangerous,
but GSP's late-fight championship
experience showed. He controlled the
octagon and finished strong. When the
fight ended, both men raised their
hands. The crowd waited, then came the
announcement. Split decision for Georges
St-Pierre, 48-47, 48-47, 48-47. The boos
were immediate and deafening. Here's
what happened. All three judges scored
two rounds to five identically. Everyone
gave Hendricks round two and four.
Everyone gave GSP rounds three and five.
The entire fight came down to round one.
D'Amato and Weeks gave the first round
to GSP. Traver gave it to Hendricks.
That was the difference between GSP
keeping his title and Hendricks becoming
champion. The problem? Almost nobody
outside those two judges thought GSP won
the first round. Then there was the
visual test. Look at the fighters. GSP's
face was battered, bruised, cut, and
swollen. Dana White was livid. At the
post-fight press conference, he said
that he was blown away that GSP won. He
went further, saying GSP knew he lost.
His corner knew that he lost. Hendricks
knew he won, and his corner knew they
won. White even called for an immediate
rematch and criticized the Nevada State
Athletic Commission for its judging
problems. Pimblett vs. Gordon. Paddy
Pimblett was on a rocket ship to
stardom. Three fights in the UFC, three
finishes, and a fan base that rivaled
some champions. He was becoming one of
the biggest draws the sport without ever
even fighting a ranked opponent. UFC 282
was supposed to be another showcase,
another highlight-reel finish, another
step towards eventual title contention.
Instead, it became one of the most
controversial decisions in recent UFC
history. From the opening moments of
round one, Gordon went to work. He
cracked Pimblett with hard left hooks
repeatedly. Pimblett's defensive
weakness, something commentators had
pointed out in previous fights, were on
full display. Gordon was landing clean,
landing often, and making Pimblett look
uncomfortable. Pimblett had some moments
with leg kicks and late flurry, but
Gordon controlled most of the action. In
the final seconds, Pimblett attempted a
takedown, but Gordon reversed it and
landed on top, drilling him with an
elbow as the round ended. Most observers
gave the first round to Gordon. Round
two was more competitive. Both fighters
had their moments. Pimblett landed some
solid kicks and briefly threatened with
a submission attempt. Gordon continued
finding success with his left hand and
landed hard shots throughout. At the end
of the round, both fighters exchanged
heavy punches. The stats were close.
Paddy Pimblett landed 28 significant
strikes to Gordon's 24, and 47 total
strikes to Gordon's 39. Gordon
controlled over two minutes of the round
on the ground after securing a takedown.
It was a toss-up round, but Pimblett
seemed to edge it slightly. The third
round is where things got messy. Gordon
shot for a takedown early and spent the
majority of the round pressing Pimblett
against the cage. He controlled nearly
four minutes of the round, grinding away
at the Liverpool fighter. Pimblett
landed a few elbows and knees with his
back against the fence, but the strike
output was minimal from both fighters.
Pimblett landed just six significant
strikes in the entire round, while
Gordon landed only four. It was ugly,
but Gordon was dictating the action and
controlling the position. When the final
bell rang, most people watching thought
Gordon had done enough. He seemed to win
round one clearly. Round three was his
with all the control time, and round two
was close enough that either fighter
could get it. Then came the decision.
Unanimous decision for Paddy Pimblett,
29-28 on all three scorecards. The boos
from the Las Vegas crowd were immediate.
Social media erupted. Fighters, media
members, and fans all called it a
robbery. At the post-fight press
conference, UFC president Dana White
didn't completely dismiss the decision,
but made an interesting comment about
Gordon's game plan. White said Gordon
had a horrible game plan in the third
round and questioned why he didn't try
to finish the fight instead of holding
Pimblett up against the cage. White
suggested that Gordon threw the round
away by assuming he'd already won the
first two. Pimblett defended the
decision afterward, telling Joe Rogan he
won the first two rounds pretty easily,
and that there was no controversy. But
Joe Rogan strongly disagreed with the
decision, even arguing with some who
thought Paddy Pimblett won.
